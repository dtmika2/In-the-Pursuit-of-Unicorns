In the Pursuit of Unicorns

Locations

The Carnival in the Whispering Wood

Location I · Opening Tutorial Tone-Setter

"The lanterns are always lit. The carousel always turns. Nobody told it to stop."

The Whispering Wood does not behave like a forest. Its trees grow inward — not a slight lean but a visible, committed angle, roots running outward, crowns bowing toward the center as if pressing toward a stage. The further in you go, the more pronounced it becomes. At the heart, the branches overhead nearly touch. Walking through the wood is walking inside something slowly closing around you.

There is no wind in the Whispering Wood. There is sound — a low, layered hum produced by every tree simultaneously, each at a slightly different pitch. Together it sits just below the threshold of language. It sounds like voices. It sounds like a name being said by many people, very quietly. It sounds like something you were about to remember.

The clearing arrives without warning. One moment the trees lean and crowd — then a break in the canopy, and light. Colored lanterns, strung between bent branches in patterns that almost make sense. A carousel, turning slowly, silently. A tent. A ticket booth. The smell of something sweet and not quite right. Nobody expected a carnival here. That is precisely the point.

Points of Interest
The CarouselDC 10 Hallucination Save to ride

The painted animals are not typical fairground creatures. There is a gnome, a unicorn, a small fairy with broken wings, and one figure at the back — too many joints, facing the wrong direction, its face worn smooth. The gnome figure's face has been carefully scratched off. Perrin does not know when that happened.

Riding triggers a Hallucination Save. On failure: the rider briefly sees the carnival populated with ghostly crowds — laughing, jostling, alive. The sound is unbearable in its beauty. On success: just the motion, the painted faces, and a strange sense of loss.

The Fortune Tent

Inside: a velvet-draped table, a deck of illustrated cards, two chairs. The cards show many illustrations but always deal the same image face-up, regardless of shuffle: a factory chimney rising against a dark sky. The card appears to have been added later. At the back of the tent, behind a curtain: a standing mirror. It reflects the room but not the party. It shows the room as it was — full of people. One of them is looking directly at whoever looks in.

The Dreamcaster using Lucid Sight on the mirror sees it clearly: a door in one of the chimneys. A way in.

The Ticket Booth

A logbook inside, fifteen years of entries in Perrin's progressively smaller handwriting. The last six months show deterioration: shorter entries, stranger observations about the trees. The final line reads: "No one came today either. The trees are very loud tonight. Something is coming. I hope it remembers what carnivals are for."

The Food TableDC 10 Hallucination Save if eaten · No Sanity cost · No lasting harm

Elaborate, perfectly preserved. Perrin laid this out years before the Night for a show about visions that never ran. He completely forgot he laced it. He will be mortified when he remembers. On fail: the party sees the carnival show that never happened — a full vivid performance, beautiful and strange. A safe, warm first encounter with the Hallucination mechanic.

The Center Pole

The tall post at the carnival's heart. All the inward-leaning trees of the wood point at it. Carved into the base, legible only if you crouch: while the night holds, what was joyful becomes compass. Perrin did not put it there. He pretends he understands it.

The Gnome — Perrin
Perrin Bliss: Tolerant · Anchored

Small, creased, smelling of cedar smoke and machine oil. He has operated this carnival for thirty years. When the Night fell he was here — front-row. The Whispering Wood's air has been in his lungs for decades; he has developed a tolerance no one can quite explain. The carnival is his anchor. As long as it turns, he stays real. If it stopped, he thinks he would drift.

He knows the party is coming. Not because he is psychic — the trees have been louder for three days. He has set out chairs. He is the warmest person the players will meet in this world. He is also very sad. He used to have crowds. They laughed because the acts were funny, not because they couldn't stop. He misses the difference more than anything.

Tutorial — What Perrin Teaches
  • The Sanity Meter · He taps his temple, counts on his fingers: "Ten feels like this. Three feels like that. Zero feels like everyone else looks." First time players see and record their Sanity 10.
  • Hallucination Saves · He offers dried moonpetal — DC 8, very mild. "Try a little. Less frightening than the accidents." On fail: visions of the world before, warm and clear. Gentle landing for the first roll of the session.
  • The Bliss · "It isn't peace. Peace is when you've solved something. This is when you've stopped caring there's something to solve."
  • The Castle on the horizon · He has seen its smoke. He believes it is magical — the source of the Night. He is wrong, and wrong in a way that matters for the revelation later.
DM Tone Note · Nothing threatens here. Nothing attacks. Let players breathe, explore, ask questions. They should leave liking Perrin. When they go, he stays. He always stays. Let the carousel still be turning when they glance back through the trees.

Mellwood

Location II · Main Hub Return Point Rest · Trade · Social

"They're all still here. Fine. Ask them — they'll tell you, through the laughter, through the tears, through the nothing behind their eyes. Everything is fine."

Mellwood is a proper village. Sixty-odd people, a well, a market square, a mill at the river's edge. It looks the way a village is supposed to look. The wrongness is entirely in the details — the baker who laughs softly to no one while he works; the child reaching calmly for the party's sharpest weapon; the man who has been sitting in the square for four days in a state of perfect content. The Bliss does not make these people evil. It makes them unreachable, each in their own particular way.

Mellwood is the party's hub. They return here after every dangerous location to rest, trade, and speak with NPCs who have more to say each time. Design it to reward coming back — the garrison opens, Aldric comes down, the hidden three emerge. None of this happens on the first visit.

The WatchtowerAldric · Lucid · Cannot descend · Key information source

Aldric has been at the top for months. He is not blissed — the elevation keeps the contaminated air below him. His legs have atrophied. He cannot descend. He lowers a bucket for supplies the villagers have mostly forgotten to fill. He survives on caught birds, rainwater, and a parapet herb garden. He is thinner than he should be and more informed than anyone else in the village.

He communicates through densely written notes. He has been watching the distant castle for months: the smoke increases at dawn and at dusk, like machinery on a schedule. He will give everything he knows to anyone who fills his bucket and looks like they haven't smiled without reason in six weeks. Getting Aldric down is a side objective — on return, with resources, it becomes possible. He becomes a genuine ally.

The Sealed FortressInaccessible · Paranoid garrison · Opens on return visit

A fortress at the village's northern edge, drawbridge up. The mechanism is on the inside. Twelve soldiers sealed themselves in six weeks ago — their Bliss manifestation is paranoia. They believe the village is compromised. They are not entirely wrong. They shout through arrow slits and will not negotiate with strangers.

What the garrison knows: one of them was a chemist's apprentice and identified the castle's smoke as industrial. They have a rough map of the approach. They have been waiting for someone who doesn't smell of Bliss.

Design two or three small "oh — that's what that meant" moments between the hidden three's observations and the other locations' discoveries. Those connections are the emotional reward for building this as a return hub.

The Blackmere

Location III Empathy Beat First Revelation

"The water is black. When you put your hand in, you see the sky. The sky is the most beautiful thing any of them have ever seen. That is the worst part."

Following the river downstream from the village, the water changes. Clear and cold at first, then darker by degrees, until it runs the color of nothing: black like void, like the river has swallowed a piece of the Night and is carrying it somewhere. The Ranger clocks the shift automatically. Others need a DC 11 Perception. The smell is faintly sweet in a wrong way — chemical, almost pleasant, almost something you'd want to breathe more of.

The river ends at the Blackmere. A lake of absolute, perfect darkness. Its surface does not move. It does not reflect. Looking into it from the bank you see nothing — no sky, no trees, no face peering back. Just the complete absence of everything. And yet it is not threatening. It is almost restful. The unicorns think so too, which is the problem.

The Water
Touching the SurfaceAutomatic · No save · Cannot be avoided

Anyone who makes contact with the Blackmere sees it immediately, without save, without choice: a bright daylit sky. Blue, enormous, warm, with clouds moving quickly and birds crossing in the distance. The sounds of day. Not a hallucination — everyone touching the water at the same moment sees the same thing. It lasts as long as contact is maintained. Stepping back ends it cleanly, as if a window closed.

The water's surface is remembering the sky it last reflected. The DM should describe this with the full weight of what it means — this may be the first time any player character has experienced daylight in this world.

Drinking the WaterDC 13 Hallucination Save · Lose 1 Sanity on fail

On Failure: the daylight does not stay at the surface. It becomes immersive — not just the sky, but the world beneath it. Green fields, moving air, warmth on skin. Beautiful and absolutely crushing in what it implies is gone. Lose 1 Sanity. The DM may whisper a hidden piece of knowledge in this vision.

On Success: the daylight vision flickers, brief and surface-level. And beneath it, visible through the brightness for just a moment: the lake bed. Pipes — dark, wide, set into the rock. Three of them, running in the direction of the distant castle. This is what is making the water black.

Class Interactions
  • Ranger · Herbalist — Automatic identification of contamination. Can trace the source upstream with a Track roll. Notes the chemical profile matches the sweetness on the air near the distant castle.
  • Dreamcaster · Lucid Sight — Sees the pipes at the lake bed clearly: three of them, a material not of this world, cold and regular and wrong.
  • Shapeshifter · Predator Sense — The unicorns' heartbeats are wrong. Too slow. Too regular. "Like something turning," if they have the words for it.
  • Sorcerer · Channel Bliss — The Bliss energy around this lake is very concentrated. Extremely high-risk, high-reward for drawing on it here.
The Unicorns
The Herd at the Shore

Six unicorns at the lake's edge. Beautiful and wrong. Their horns are dim — not lightless, but far below what they should be, the way a lamp looks in the middle of the day. Their movements are slow and effortful, as though the air around them is slightly thicker. They are drinking from the black water. They cannot stop. The Bliss draws them here. They cannot name the compulsion. They simply return, again and again, to the shore that is quietly hurting them.

They do not flee. They do not display fear. They turn their heads with calm, slow eyes — calm in a way that is very different from peaceful. They allow themselves to be touched. When a hand is placed on a flank, the animal leans into it very slightly. Not in joy. In something that remembers joy and cannot quite find where it went.

The Moment of ContactRoleplay-triggered · Optional DC 8 Hallucination Save

If a character is patient and genuinely kind in their approach — the DM's call, based on roleplay — one unicorn will press its head against them. Its horn glows, briefly. A small warmth, like catching sunlight through a window.

The character may take a voluntary DC 8 Hallucination Save. On any result, they see a flash from the unicorn's memory: running through a sunlit field at full speed, the world green and enormous. And then a smell arriving. The light going away. The field getting slower. Getting further. And then this shore, and this water, and the wanting to drink, and the not knowing why.

There is no mechanical consequence beyond what the DM adds. Its purpose is to give the players something to carry. They are not saving a world in the abstract. They have now met something specific that is being ruined. That difference matters.

The Horizon
The Silhouette Across the LakeDC 16 Perception · Dreamcaster sees truth · Bliss Root: both at once

On the far shore, beyond hills that slope into fog: a silhouette. At this distance it reads as a castle — dark towers, stone battlements, smoke that looks like clouds caught on the highest parapet. It is exactly what it looks like, and it is nothing of the sort.

DC 16 Perception: something is wrong with the shape. The edges are too clean. The "towers" are too evenly spaced. Stone does not sit quite like that.

Dreamcaster · Lucid Sight: sees it completely. Glass, steel, ventilation stacks. The smoke is exhaust. The spires are chimneys. The Dreamcaster must decide what to tell the others, and how, and how fast.

Bliss Root consumed here: both images occupy the same space simultaneously — the castle and the factory, flickering between each other, neither fully stable. The truth visible beneath the lie for a few seconds at a time. Lose 1 Sanity. The DM may use this as the session's first hard revelation.

DM Tone Note · This location has the weight of a nature documentary — the kind where you watch something being slowly destroyed and cannot look away. The unicorns are not in immediate danger. They are in the slow danger of something being done to them that they cannot understand and the party cannot fix yet. Let that settle before moving on. The players should leave the Blackmere with a clear emotional reason to keep going.

The Lamp-Hvozd

Location IV · Combat Artifacts → Return to Mellwood

"A forest of iron poles and glass lanterns. No flame inside them. No hum. They simply glow, the way trees simply grow — as if this is what they have always done, and always will."

It announces itself as a glow on the horizon before the trees even thin. Walking toward it, warm light filters between normal trunks. Then the normal trees thin. Then there are no normal trees. There are only lamps: iron poles, five to fifteen feet tall, each topped with a glass lantern. Hundreds of them, in no discernible order, spaced as randomly as trees in a natural forest. Their light is steady and makes no sound. There is no electrical hum. There is no visible flame. They simply glow.

The effect is deeply uncanny and, despite everything, deeply calming. The space between the poles is like forest understory — soft ground, the occasional root, light filtered from above. But the light comes from iron, and the iron goes on in every direction, and this hvozd — this ancient, dense, dark forest — is made entirely of things that should not grow here.

The truth, long forgotten: the factory's first outdoor electrical test array. Workers placed the lamps across the countryside to test which areas received reliable power from the generators. The test succeeded. The workers left. Everyone forgot. The lamps have been running for years on power they draw from a cable buried just below the topsoil, still connected, still fed by the factory's output.

The Inverses — Shadow Creatures
What They Are

The Inverses do not have their own form. What the party sees, when the creatures emerge, is themselves.

The Inverses observe from shadow before combat begins — studying each party member carefully, copying their appearance with perfect fidelity. Same face. Same clothing. Same weapon, same posture, same way of standing. The copies are visually exact. They do not speak. They move with a stillness between steps, like a portrait that has learned to walk.

The horror happens before the first attack. DM narration: "You notice [character name] walking just a few feet to your left — same coat, same weapon. You don't think much of it. And then you look ahead, and [character name] is already there, talking to someone else. You look back. The thing wearing [character name]'s face slowly turns its head toward you. And then it attacks."

The tells, visible on close inspection: no shadow of their own (ironic, given what they are), eyes that are flat and wrong — painted rather than alive — and absolute silence. No breath. No voice. On first contact, none of this is obvious from a distance. In the lamp light and the confusion, they are indistinguishable.

DC 13 Perception or Insight to distinguish an Inverse from a real party member before engaging. Automatic for the Ranger using Predator Sense — they smell of nothing, where a person should be. Automatic for the Dreamcaster who attempts Telepathy — there is no mind to find. For everyone else, the wraith-vision holds until the check is made or the Inverse attacks first.

The Burst
When the Last Inverse Falls

A moment of absolute stillness. The Lamp-Hvozd holds its breath.

Then, beginning from the furthest visible lamp and traveling inward like a slow wave: every lamp cracks. The glass fractures from base to top. The iron splits. The bulbs shatter in a silent sequence, moving toward the party's center. It takes about ten seconds. It is very beautiful and slightly terrifying.

And the light stays.

It rises from each shattered lamp as a small hovering orb — warm, still, soft, slightly golden. The Lamp-Hvozd is darker in some ways (no lamp fixtures) and lighter in others (the light moves freely, drifting at head height, filling the space evenly without source or shadow). The orbs drift slowly toward the party as if curious. A few follow them when they eventually leave — a gentle, warm escort through the treeline that fades after a few hundred meters.

In the debris at the base of each shattered lamp: one item, found by the character nearest to it at the moment of the burst. The items were inside the lamps — preserved for years in glass, unreachable until now.

The artifacts from the burst lamps are not new items. Each lamp contains a refracted copy of what the character nearest to it already carries — the same weapon, the same focus, the same tool — reforged in crystallized lamp-glass. Slightly better than what they had before. As if the lamps spent years watching this world and prepared accordingly.

After the Burst — Story Return
  • The party is intact and has new artifacts. The floating light orbs follow them a short distance through the trees — a gentle escort.
  • This is a natural return beat to Mellwood: rest, process what happened, interact with NPCs who have new things to say now that the party has been to another dangerous place.
  • The Lamp-Hvozd is now permanently different. If the party returns to it later, the lamps are gone and the floating orbs still drift between the iron poles. The Inverses are not replaced.
DM Tone Note · The Lamp-Hvozd is the session's one uncomplicated win — a real combat encounter with a beautiful and surprising payoff. Play it with a slightly lighter hand than the other locations. The artifacts should feel like gifts. The floating lights should feel like the world saying thank you in the only language it still has. Let the players have this before things get harder.

The Sinking Station

Location V · Trap Zone Loot → Return to Mellwood

"Someone was here. Someone who knew what was happening, who understood it better than anyone, who was this close to stopping it. And then the Bliss got them too. And then no one came to check."

A partially collapsed hatch in a hillside, half-covered with roots and soil. Easy to miss — DC 12 Perception — but automatic for the Ranger, who recognizes the metal framework as manufactured rather than natural. Below: a three-chamber monitoring facility, built by the factory in its earliest days to track how the Bliss compound spread through soil and groundwater.

The facility was staffed by one researcher. When their notes became erratic and then stopped, the factory sent no one to check. What the researcher was officially meant to do — log dispersal rates — they did. What they were unofficially doing in their last weeks — trying to find a reversal — was far more valuable. They were close. They did not make it. What they left behind is the most useful collection of objects the party will find before the factory.

The whole structure has shifted — the hill above has settled unevenly over the years. Floors tilt by five or ten degrees. Doorframes are slightly wrong. Nothing is dangerous on its own, but the disorientation is real and the traps, both factory-installed and environment-created, are still active.

Chamber I — The Entry Hall
Description

Originally a decontamination and storage room. Metal lockers line the walls, several bent open by the settling of the structure. A pegboard with hooks, most empty. The floor tilts about eight degrees toward the far wall — not enough to slip on, but enough to notice. The air smells of rust and something sweetly chemical.

Traps
T1Phonograph alarm. A pressure plate near the entry ladder triggers a phonograph mechanism in the ceiling — a piercing mechanical shriek that lasts 6 seconds, audible throughout the station. DC 13 Perception to spot the plate before stepping on it. No damage, but any creature still in the station is alerted. The Ranger detects it automatically.
T2Booby-trapped locker. One locker (DC 12 Perception to notice the wire) releases a small Bliss-compound canister when opened — a concentrated burst. DC 12 Hallucination Save for anyone within 5 feet, no Sanity loss but a full vision that lasts one round and leaves them Dazed (disadvantage on their next action). The Ranger's Herbalist ability: automatic detection if they examine the locker before opening.
Loot

Two lockers are untrapped and unlocked — one contains 50 feet of quality rope. Another holds a pair of protective goggles (thick glass lenses, slightly scratched — grant +1 to Hallucination Saves caused by visual effects only). A third locker is locked (DC 11 to pick or force) — inside, nothing of value except a researcher's personal effects: a photograph of a family, a pressed flower, a small notebook of shopping lists. The shopping lists end mid-item six months ago.

Chamber II — The Monitoring Room
Description

The main work space. Banks of gauges and dials bolted to the walls, most cracked or dead. A worktable with a chair lying on its side. Papers everywhere — fallen from a toppled filing cabinet, water-damaged and scattered, but many still readable. A map is pinned to the far wall, partially torn. The floor here is the most tilted — nearly 12 degrees. Things left on surfaces have slid to the lower wall.

Traps
T3Electrified floor panel. A section of floor (roughly 3x3 feet) still carries live current from the factory's underground cable. It looks slightly different — a faint shimmer, a discoloration, a smell of ozone. DC 13 Perception to notice. Stepping on it: 1d6 lightning damage, DC 12 Dexterity save or knocked prone. The Ranger and any character carrying the Filament Thread from the Lamp-Hvozd recognizes the smell of live electricity immediately.
T4Overloaded shelf. A wall-mounted shelf holds water-logged paper files, far beyond its structural limit. Any character who walks directly beneath it (DC 11 Perception to notice the creak) or disturbs the wall triggers it — the shelf collapses. 1d4 bludgeoning damage, DC 12 Dexterity save to dodge entirely. The real hazard: the noise, and the papers now burying the floor, making T3's panel harder to see (DC 15 Perception to spot it beneath the fallen paper).
Loot

The partial factory map pinned to the wall is the most important item here — a hand-drawn exterior survey of the castle-factory, annotated with the researcher's observations about smoke timing, guard movement (there is guard movement), and one circled entry point labeled "the service gate — unmonitored at dusk." The map is torn, missing the interior, but invaluable for the approach.

In a locked cabinet (DC 11): three vials of anti-Bliss compound, the researcher's own formulation — crude but effective. Each grants advantage on one Hallucination Save. The label reads: not a cure. a moment of clarity. do not take more than one.

In a drawer: two signal flares. They are specifically calibrated to be visible from Mellwood's watchtower. Using one will prompt a response from Aldric — a note in his bucket, lowered the next time the party passes through. Aldric made these. He sent them here. He was hoping someone would find them. He's been waiting for a long time.

Chamber III — The Research Room
Description

The researcher's personal space. A cot, unmade. A desk, covered in notes. Shelves of glass specimen jars — preserved plants, insects, soil samples labeled with dates and grid coordinates. A half-eaten meal, fully petrified by time. A coat still hanging by the door. On the desk: an open journal, pen still resting in the groove of the binding as if the researcher just stepped away.

Traps
T5Living specimen jar. One specimen shelf holds jars that are mostly inert — all except one, which contains a small Inverse, preserved but still alive, its shadow pressing against the glass. If the jar is broken, dropped, or the shelf is disturbed heavily (DC 12 Perception to identify which jar before touching the shelf), the Inverse emerges. It is disoriented, has only 5 HP, deals 1d4 damage on a hit, and flees after 2 rounds — through a gap in the ceiling too small for anyone to follow. It is more surprise than threat. The sound of the jar breaking is significant.
T6Root incursion. Tree roots have grown through the ceiling over the years and dangle at head height throughout the room. They absorbed Bliss compound from the soil above. Grabbing them, walking into them carelessly, or disturbing them causes them to release a small amount of absorbed compound — a faint mist. Anyone in the room when this happens must make a DC 10 Hallucination Save or see the room as it was when occupied: the researcher at the desk, alive, writing. The vision lasts one round. No Sanity loss — just vivid and disorienting. The Ranger can identify the roots before they cause this.
The Researcher's JournalThe most valuable item in the station

147 pages, varying handwriting — neat at the start, progressively more hurried, then disturbingly calm in the final entries as the Bliss took hold. Key contents:

Weeks 1–8: dispersal logs, exactly as commissioned. Precise and professional. Includes a complete formula for the Bliss compound — the first time the party can see its chemical nature described plainly.

Weeks 9–16: the researcher begins to notice something is wrong. Correlates their dispersal maps with behavioral reports from nearby villages. Realizes the compound is not a side effect — it is the purpose. Their tone shifts. They begin working on a neutralizing agent.

Weeks 17–22: the neutralizing agent is close. Three promising formulations are described in detail. Sable the healer (the hidden three in the cave) can read these and possibly complete the work given time, supplies, and Marta the Herbalist's knowledge. The researcher was interrupted before the fourth attempt.

Final entry: "The control room has two confirmation switches, both must be held simultaneously to initiate shutdown. I could not find a way to reach both alone. I spent a long time on that. I think I am getting happy. I am going to lie down. I hope whoever finds this is not alone."
